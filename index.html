1.0 – Course Introduction

This module introduces the structure and expectations of the Sick Call Screener Course.

1.1 – Scope of Care

The Sick Call Screener Program exists to provide timely, high‑quality care for active‑duty personnel with minor medical conditions. Sick Call Screeners (SCS) do not function as independent providers. They operate under the direct supervision of a Medical Officer (MO), Physician Assistant (PA), Nurse Practitioner (NP), or Independent Duty Corpsman (IDC).

Patient Flow & Sick Call Operations

  • Patients check in at the front desk and present a valid DoD ID card.
  • Clerks log the visit according to local SOP and retrieve the medical record.
  • Patients are directed to the waiting or screening area and are generally seen in order of arrival.
  • Personnel must be able to identify patients requiring immediate care and notify the supervising provider without delay.
  • Routine patients receive vital signs and an initial complaint review by qualified enlisted medical personnel.

Screener Responsibilities

  • Conduct patient interviews and follow approved procedures.
  • Recognize personal limitations and seek assistance whenever doubt exists.
  • Patients may request to be seen by an MO/PA/NP/IDC at any time.

Scope of Care Guidelines

  • Use the SOAP format for every patient encounter.
  • Consult the supervising provider before the patient leaves the treatment facility.
  • 100% of SCS records must be reviewed and countersigned by the supervising provider.
  • Screeners may order labs only when allowed by local policy; all others require provider approval.
  • Screeners may evaluate and treat only minor illnesses with approved protocols and direct supervision.

Minor Illnesses Screeners May Treat

  • Minor headaches
  • Uncomplicated upper respiratory infections
  • Uncomplicated ENT conditions
  • Minor dermatological conditions
  • Minor musculoskeletal problems
  • Uncomplicated gastrointestinal conditions
  • Minor wounds not requiring suturing or complex care
  • Hypertension (serial BP checks only, when ordered)
  • Suture removal
  • Staple removal

Assisting With Conditions Outside Scope

Screeners may assist the medical provider with conditions beyond their scope, including:

  • General HEENT, respiratory, cardiovascular, GI, neurological, musculoskeletal, dermatological, endocrine, and environmental complaints
  • Mental health concerns
  • Any situation where only history and basic physical exam can be obtained

Immediate Referral Criteria

The following require immediate referral to an MO/PA/NP/IDC:

  • Fever ≥ 103°F, or >100.4°F for 48 hours
  • Respiratory rate > 28/min without cause
  • Pulse > 120/min without cause
  • Persistent diastolic BP > 105 mmHg over 3 days
  • Suspected infectious diseases (e.g., chickenpox, TB)
  • Chest pain of possible cardiac origin
  • Abdominal pain with fever or worsening symptoms
  • Bleeding, hematemesis, hemoptysis, hematochezia
  • Sudden testicular pain (possible torsion)
  • Loss of consciousness
  • Any degree of airway compromise
  • Return visits for unresolved complaints
  • Eye injuries, limb loss, significant visual changes
  • Altered mental status with suicidal/homicidal ideation
  • Loss of peripheral pulse
  • Tendon, nerve, or vessel damage
  • Capillary refill > 3 seconds with acute injury
  • Pain > 7/10
  • Open fractures, amputations, deformities, joint penetration

Referral to Higher Level of Care

  • Dermatitis, rashes, skin irritations
  • Vaccinations
  • UTIs, bladder infections, yeast infections
  • Ear infections
  • Diagnostic labs/tests
  • Physicals and wellness exams
  • Health screenings
  • Medication refills
  • Abnormal vital signs
  • Signs of infection (redness, pus)
  • Contaminated wounds
  • Animal/human bites
  • Puncture wounds
  • Any condition outside SCS comfort or protocol

Key Principles

  • This course does not train independent providers.
  • Screeners must always work under MO/PA/NP/IDC supervision.

1.2 – Ethics

Introduction

Ethics form the foundation of professional conduct in military medicine. Hospital Corpsmen must uphold the highest standards of integrity, compassion, and responsibility while caring for service members in both routine and operational environments.

The Hospital Corpsman Pledge

“I solemnly pledge myself before God and these witnesses to practice faithfully all of my duties as a member of the Hospital Corps. I hold the care of the sick and injured to be a privilege and a sacred trust and will assist the Medical Department Officer with loyalty and honesty. I will not knowingly permit harm to come to any patient. I will not partake of nor administer any unauthorized medication. I will hold all personal matters pertaining to the private lives of patients in strict confidence. I dedicate my heart, mind and strength to the work before me. I shall do all within my power to show in myself an example of all that is honorable and good throughout my Naval career.”

Universal Medical Ethics Principles

  • Autonomy: The right of competent individuals to make informed decisions free from coercion. In healthcare, this is expressed through informed consent.
  • Beneficence: Acting in the patient’s best interest and promoting their health. May sometimes conflict with autonomy.
  • Nonmaleficence: “Do no harm.” Avoiding unnecessary or unacceptable burdens on the patient.
  • Justice: Treating similar cases similarly and applying fair, consistent standards.

The Concept of Moral Injury

Military personnel may face ethical and moral challenges in combat zones. Even with training, leadership, and clear rules of engagement, exposure to traumatic or morally troubling events can undermine a service member’s sense of humanity.

A moral injury occurs when an individual experiences or participates in an act that violates deeply held moral or ethical beliefs, leading to significant inner conflict.

Ethical Issues in Military Medical Settings

  • The Military Health System (MHS) spans combat medicine, peacetime care, public health, training, and research.
  • Military healthcare professionals face all civilian ethical dilemmas, plus additional challenges unique to military operations.
  • Ethical conflicts may require support from the chain of command; mission requirements may limit provider autonomy.

Ethical Decision-Making

Ethical decisions often involve deeply held beliefs about life, health, and duty. Conflicts may arise between personal conscience and professional obligations.

  • Respect for conscience must be balanced with patient rights and access to care.
  • Providers must refer patients in a timely manner if they cannot provide requested services due to moral objections.
  • In emergencies, providers must deliver medically indicated care regardless of personal beliefs.
  • Conscience-based withdrawal from care must not interfere with patient access to services.

Ethics Education and Training

  • Stimulate moral imagination: Understand the experiences and emotions behind ethical choices.
  • Recognize ethical issues: Identify moral dimensions in clinical situations.
  • Develop analytical skills: Construct logical, defensible arguments in the face of disagreement.
  • Sense of moral obligation: Replace assumptions with thoughtful, responsible decision-making.
  • Cope with moral ambiguity: Accept that ethical problems often lack clear answers.

Summary and Review

Ethics guide the conduct of military medical professionals. Corpsmen must uphold autonomy, beneficence, nonmaleficence, and justice while navigating unique military challenges. Understanding moral injury, ethical decision-making, and the responsibilities of military medicine ensures the highest standard of care.

1.3 – SOAP Note

Introduction

The SOAP note is the standard format for documenting patient encounters in medical treatment records. It ensures clear communication, supports continuity of care, and provides a structured method for follow-up evaluation. This lesson helps experienced Corpsmen refine their documentation skills and guides less experienced Corpsmen in producing accurate, complete notes.

S – Subjective

The Subjective section contains information gathered from interviewing the patient. Direct quotes are helpful. Use a sequential approach to gather a complete history.

Chief Complaint (CC)

  • “What is your reason for seeking care?”
  • “What can I do for you?”

History of Present Illness (HPI)

  • Onset: When symptoms began; sudden or gradual.
  • Location: Where the pain or symptom is; does it radiate?
  • Duration: How long symptoms last; constant or intermittent.
  • Character: Description of symptoms (sharp, dull, burning, etc.).
  • Aggravating/Associated Factors: What worsens symptoms; other related symptoms.
  • Relieving Factors: What improves symptoms and how.
  • Temporal Factors: Pattern or timing of symptoms.
  • Severity: Pain scale 0–10; impact on daily activities.

Past Medical History (PMHx)

  • Hospitalizations or surgeries (dates, reasons).
  • Serious illnesses or injuries.
  • Current, recent, or past medications (including OTC, supplements, recreational drugs).
  • Allergies (drug, food, environmental).

Family History (FHx)

  • Major health or genetic disorders in the family.

Social History (SHx)

  • Alcohol use.
  • Exercise habits.
  • Tobacco/vape use.
  • Nutrition (caffeine, salt intake).
  • Sleep patterns.
  • Work stress and anxiety.
  • Education level.
  • Occupation.
  • Religious barriers to care.
  • Travel/deployment history.
  • Sexual history and contraception.

Depression Screening

Ask about symptoms over the last two weeks, including:

  • Loss of interest or pleasure.
  • Feeling down or hopeless.
  • Sleep disturbances.
  • Low energy.
  • Appetite changes.
  • Feelings of worthlessness.
  • Difficulty concentrating.
  • Psychomotor changes.
  • Thoughts of self-harm.

Suicidal/Homicidal Ideation (SI/HI)

Screen early using the Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale. Ask:

  • “Have you wished you were dead or could go to sleep and not wake up?”
  • “Have you had thoughts of killing yourself or others?”
  • “Have you thought about how you might do it?”
  • “Do you intend to act on these thoughts?”
  • “Have you made any preparations or attempts?”

Review of Systems (ROS)

Screen for symptoms across body systems:

  • Constitutional: Fever, chills, fatigue, weight changes.
  • Skin/Hair/Nails: Rash, itching, changes in texture or growth.
  • Blood/Lymph: Frequent infections, bruising, lymph node issues.
  • Head/Neck: Headaches, dizziness, neck stiffness.
  • Eyes: Vision changes, pain, photophobia.
  • Ears: Hearing loss, tinnitus, vertigo.
  • Nose: Congestion, epistaxis, sinus pain.
  • Throat/Mouth: Sore throat, ulcers, dental issues.
  • Lymph Nodes: Enlargement or tenderness.
  • Chest/Lungs: Dyspnea, wheezing, cough, hemoptysis.
  • Cardiovascular: Chest pain, palpitations, edema.
  • Breasts: Pain, lumps, discharge.
  • Gastrointestinal: Nausea, vomiting, bowel changes, jaundice.
  • Genitourinary: Dysuria, hematuria, frequency, discharge.
  • Musculoskeletal: Joint pain, swelling, limited motion.
  • Neurological: Seizures, weakness, numbness, tremors.
  • Psychological: Depression, anxiety, irritability.
  • Male: Erectile issues, discharge, testicular pain.
  • Female: Menstrual history, abnormal bleeding, pregnancy history.

O – Objective

The Objective section includes all observations and measurable findings.

  • General appearance: First impression of the patient.
  • Signs of distress: Respiratory effort, pain, mental status.
  • Physical findings: Pertinent positives and negatives.
  • Vital signs: BP, HR, RR, Temp, SpO₂.
  • Relevant labs: From current or previous encounters.
  • Relevant imaging: X-rays or other studies.

A – Assessment

The Assessment is the provider’s interpretation of the history and physical exam. It may include one or multiple diagnoses, differential considerations, and clinical impressions.

P – Plan

The Plan outlines the next steps in patient care, including:

  • Medications: What was prescribed.
  • Treatments: Interventions performed.
  • Physical limitations: Duty status or restrictions.
  • Follow-up: When and where the patient should return.
  • Ancillary tests: X-rays, labs, or other diagnostics.
  • Patient education: Medication instructions, self-care, printed materials, and patient understanding.
  • Disposition: Duty, profile, quarters, or referrals.

1.4 – Patient Presentation

Introduction

A patient presentation is the structured communication of pertinent history, findings, and clinical impressions from the Corpsman to the supervising provider. Effective presentations ensure safe handoff, continuity of care, and accurate clinical decision-making.

Purpose of a Patient Presentation

  • Communicates essential patient information clearly and efficiently.
  • Supports safe transfer of care between healthcare professionals.
  • Ensures continuity of care and enhances patient safety.

How to Present a Patient

  • Identify yourself and establish rapport with the provider.
  • Begin with general patient information: rank/title, name, age, and gender.
  • State the chief complaint clearly.
  • Summarize the History of Present Illness (HPI) using the OLDCARTS mnemonic.
  • Discuss relevant past medical, surgical, family, and social history.
  • Report pertinent positive and negative findings from the Review of Systems (ROS).
  • Present key findings from your physical examination, including:
    • General impression: distress, mental status, deformities, gait, body language.
    • System-by-system findings in a logical, head-to-toe sequence.
    • Vital signs, noting if BP and pulse were taken manually.
  • Provide your assessment based on history and exam. Be prepared to offer possible differentials.
  • Report your developing plan, which may include:
    • Patient education and reassurance.
    • Medications or therapies initiated.
    • Rest and sleep recommendations.
    • Supportive therapies (e.g., stretches, saltwater gargles).
    • Follow-up instructions for worsening or persistent symptoms.
    • Duty status or disposition (light duty, SIQ, full duty).

Barriers to Effective Reporting

Common obstacles that reduce clarity and effectiveness include:

  • Providing unnecessary or irrelevant information.
  • Giving overly lengthy or disorganized reports.
  • Language or communication barriers.
  • Missing key information.
  • Lack of a standardized reporting format.
  • Interruptions during the presentation.

A systematic, structured approach is essential for delivering a clear and complete patient presentation.

Summary and Review

A well-organized patient presentation ensures accurate communication, supports provider decision-making, and enhances patient safety. Mastery of this skill is essential for all Sick Call Screeners.
